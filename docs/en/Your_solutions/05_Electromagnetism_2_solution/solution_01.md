# 🌌 Physics Basics: Understanding Gauss's Law

## 1. Problem Overview
In this problem, we are tasked with calculating the **total electric flux** ($\Phi_E$) generated by a single point charge located at the center of a spherical surface.

**Given Data:**
* **Point Charge ($q$):** $+2.0 \, \text{C}$
* **Location:** Origin $(0,0,0)$
* **Gaussian Surface:** A sphere with radius $r = 1.0 \, \text{m}$ centered at the origin.

---

## 2. Fundamental Principle: Gauss's Law
Gauss's Law is one of the four Maxwell's equations and provides a powerful way to relate electric fields to their sources (charges).

The law states that the net electric flux through any **closed surface** is proportional to the total charge enclosed within that surface.

### The Mathematical Formula:
$$\Phi_E = \oint \vec{E} \cdot d\vec{A} = \frac{Q_{enclosed}}{\varepsilon_0}$$

Where:
* **$\Phi_E$**: Total Electric Flux (Unit: $N \cdot m^2/C$ or $V \cdot m$)
* **$Q_{enclosed}$**: The net charge inside the surface.
* **$\varepsilon_0$**: Permittivity of free space ($\approx 8.854 \times 10^{-12} \, \text{C}^2/\text{N}\cdot\text{m}^2$)

---

## 3. Conceptual Analysis (The "Radius" Secret)

One of the most important concepts in this "Basics" course is understanding why the **radius ($1 \, \text{m}$)** is mentioned but not used in the final calculation.

[Image of electric field lines from a point charge through a sphere]

### Why doesn't the radius matter?
Think of the electric flux as the **total number of electric field lines** leaving the charge. 
1. If you have a small sphere around the charge, all lines must pass through it.
2. If you have a much larger sphere, those same lines still have to pass through it eventually.

Since the total number of lines (flux) depends only on the "strength" of the source (the charge), the size of the sphere does not change the **total** flux. It only changes the **density** of the lines on the surface (which is the Electric Field $E$).

---

## 4. Step-by-Step Solution

### Step 1: Identify the Enclosed Charge
The problem states the charge is at the origin and the sphere is centered at the origin. Therefore, the entire charge is inside our "Gaussian Surface."
$$Q_{enclosed} = 2.0 \, \text{C}$$

### Step 2: Set Up the Constant
We use the standard value for the permittivity of vacuum:
$$\varepsilon_0 \approx 8.85 \times 10^{-12} \, \frac{\text{C}^2}{\text{N} \cdot \text{m}^2}$$

### Step 3: Calculation
Plug the values into the Gauss's Law formula:
$$\Phi_E = \frac{2.0}{8.85 \times 10^{-12}}$$

Using scientific notation:
$$\Phi_E \approx 2.25988 \times 10^{11}$$

Rounding to significant figures:
$$\Phi_E \approx 2.26 \times 10^{11} \, \text{N} \cdot \text{m}^2/\text{C}$$

---

## 5. Conclusion
The total electric flux through a spherical surface of radius $1 \, \text{m}$ enclosing a $+2 \, \text{C}$ charge is **$2.26 \times 10^{11} \, \text{V} \cdot \text{m}$**.
