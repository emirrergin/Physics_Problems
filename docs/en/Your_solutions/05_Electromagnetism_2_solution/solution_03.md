# ⚡ Physics Basics: Biot-Savart Law

## 1. Problem Statement
A small segment of a line wire of length **$0.1 \, \text{m}$** carries a current of **$3 \, \text{A}$**. The segment is located at a distance of **$0.2 \, \text{m}$** from a point **$P$**. Calculate the magnetic field at point $P$ due to this current segment, assuming the segment is perpendicular to the line connecting it to point $P$.

---

## 2. Fundamental Physics Principle: Biot-Savart Law
The Biot-Savart Law describes the magnetic field ($B$) generated by a constant electric current. For a very small segment of wire (a "current element"), the magnitude of the magnetic field at a point is given by:

$$B = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \frac{I \cdot \Delta L \cdot \sin(\theta)}{r^2}$$

Where:
* **$\mu_0$**: Permeability of free space ($4\pi \times 10^{-7} \, \text{T}\cdot\text{m/A}$)
* **$I$**: Current in Amperes ($A$)
* **$\Delta L$**: Length of the small segment ($m$)
* **$r$**: Distance from the segment to the point ($m$)
* **$\theta$**: The angle between the segment and the line to point $P$.

---

## 3. Given Information
* **Current ($I$):** $3 \, \text{A}$
* **Segment Length ($\Delta L$):** $0.1 \, \text{m}$
* **Distance ($r$):** $0.2 \, \text{m}$
* **Angle ($\theta$):** $90^\circ$ (Because the segment is "perpendicular").
* **Constant ($\frac{\mu_0}{4\pi}$):** $10^{-7} \, \text{T}\cdot\text{m/A}$

---

## 4. Step-by-Step Calculation

### Step 1: Identify the Sine Value
Since the segment is perpendicular to the point $P$:
$$\sin(90^\circ) = 1$$

### Step 2: Substitute Values into the Formula
$$B = (10^{-7}) \cdot \frac{3 \cdot 0.1 \cdot 1}{(0.2)^2}$$

### Step 3: Simplify the Expression
First, calculate the denominator:
$$(0.2)^2 = 0.04$$

Now, calculate the numerator:
$$3 \cdot 0.1 = 0.3$$

Divide numerator by denominator:
$$\frac{0.3}{0.04} = 7.5$$

### Step 4: Final Calculation
$$B = 10^{-7} \cdot 7.5$$
$$B = 7.5 \times 10^{-7} \, \text{T}$$

---

## 5. Final Result
The magnetic field at point $P$ is **$7.5 \times 10^{-7} \, \text{Tesla}$** (or $0.75 \, \mu\text{T}$).
